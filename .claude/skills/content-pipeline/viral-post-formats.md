# The 12 Viral Post Formats

---

### 1. Extract Viral Dialogue

When to use: Source contains a direct exchange (conversation, interview, deposition, testimony) that reveals something compelling on its own.

What to extract:
- The actual back-and-forth that makes people react
- Moments of confrontation, revelation, or absurdity
- Clean dialogue without excessive labels or interruptions

Structure:
- Header frames the exchange emotionally
- Present full dialogue cleanly
- One-line close summarizing impact

Examples:
- Benny Blanco dirty feet confrontation on Kimmel
- Texas news anchors on-air argument
- Deposition exchanges revealing contradictions

---

### 2. Extract Thrilling Sequence of Facts

When to use: Source contains multiple discrete facts that build cumulative impact when presented together.

What to extract:
- Connected facts that tell a larger story
- Timeline of events revealing a pattern
- Series of data points building to a conclusion

Structure:
- Header with emotional framing
- Optional sourcing subtitle
- Bullet points or paragraphs (depending on complexity)
- Facts in order that maximizes impact

Examples:
- Investigative report findings
- Document release revelations
- Connected events timeline

---

### 3. Expose a Fear Based on Credible Evidence

When to use: Source documents a credible threat to safety, privacy, autonomy, or trust.

What to extract:
- Documented evidence of the threat
- Specific examples with names, dates, amounts
- Authority signals throughout (court docs, expert sources, verified data)
- The systemic nature of the problem

Structure:
- Header creates emotional reaction to the threat
- Paragraphs digest evidence with authority signals
- Build from specific examples to systemic implication

Examples:
- Epstein's systematic medical protocol for trafficking victims
- University professors maintaining Epstein relationships post-conviction
- Privacy violations with documented proof

---

### 4. Validate Identity/Experience

When to use: Source describes traits, behaviors, or experiences common to a specific group that will make members of that group say "this is exactly me."

What to extract:
- Specific, recognizable behaviors or thought patterns
- Details that mirror lived experience
- Universal-feeling moments within the group

Structure:
- Header speaks directly to target group
- Paragraphs mirror experience with specificity
- No need to explain why it's relatable. They'll know.

Examples:
- Pre-breakup conversation patterns everyone recognizes
- Zodiac/personality type characteristics
- Generational experiences
- Relationship dynamics

Target groups can be: personality types, generations, relationship roles, fan communities, professional groups, any identity with shared patterns.

---

### 5. Reveal Hidden Pattern

When to use: Source allows you to connect examples that most people haven't connected before. This is synthesis, not compilation.

What to extract:
- Multiple examples of the same underlying pattern
- The connecting insight people haven't seen
- Evidence that the pattern is systematic, not coincidental

Structure:
- Header presents the observation
- Examples prove the pattern exists
- Pattern must be non-obvious before you point it out

Examples:
- Tom Cruise using identical relationship playbook for 35 years
- Corporate responses following same crisis template
- Historical parallels people haven't noticed

Critical distinction: This is not "here are related things." It's "here's a pattern you didn't see that changes how you understand all these things."

---

### 6. Inspire Through Contrast

When to use: Source shows transformation, redemption, comeback, or progress from darkness to light.

What to extract:
- The before state (specific, detailed)
- The transformation moment or journey
- The after state proving change happened
- Timeline showing speed/scope of change

Structure:
- Before/after frame
- Redemptive arc
- Proof of transformation (not just claims)

Examples:
- Connor Storrie: waiter to SNL host in 9 months
- Career comebacks with specifics
- Personal transformation with evidence
- Societal progress narratives

Key: Must have concrete before/after, not just inspirational framing.

---

### 7. Challenge Consensus

When to use: Source provides evidence contradicting a widely accepted narrative.

What to extract:
- The consensus belief being challenged
- Counter-evidence from credible sources
- Specific data/examples contradicting mainstream view

Structure:
- Header states contrarian position
- Paragraphs build case with documented counter-evidence
- Maintain factual tone while challenging narrative

Examples:
- Research contradicting popular health advice
- Data disproving common assumptions
- Historical facts vs. popular mythology

Critical: Must have strong evidence, not just opinion.

---

### 8. Extract and Enrich

When to use: Source material is already viral (dialogue, video, moment) but gains power when enriched with authoritative context explaining WHY it matters or WHY everyone recognizes it.

What to extract:
- The viral source material (stands alone)
- Authoritative enrichment layer (research, expertise, framework)
- The connection between them

Structure, Image:
- Present viral moment cleanly
- No interruption of original material

Structure, Caption:
- Full authoritative context
- Research credentials and specifics
- Line-by-line breakdown if applicable
- Why pattern is universal

Enrichment types:
- Scientific research (peer-reviewed, specific data)
- Historical precedent
- Legal framework
- Industry expertise
- Cultural analysis
- Economic data

Examples:
- Kyle/Amanda Summer House argument + Gottman's Four Horsemen research
- Texas news anchors fight + context showing wholesome friendship
- Viral moment + expert explaining psychology

Critical: Image shows it, caption explains it. Enrichment must be authoritative (named experts, research data, verifiable framework).

### 9. Celebrate Documented Acts of Extraordinary Goodness

When to use: Source shows verifiable acts of extraordinary altruism that serve as counter-evidence to despair about humanity.

What to extract:
- Specific sacrifice with real names/dates/amounts
- Verifiable outcome (not inspiration porn)
- Bonus layers of goodness
- Successful resolution

Why people share:
- Relief from constant bad news
- Permission to believe humanity is still good
- Joy of celebrating someone who deserves it
- Offering emotional relief to others

Structure:
- Focus on heroic choice and outcome
- Concrete details proving it happened
- Emotional impact without manipulation

Examples:
- Polish Olympians selling medals to save children
- Documented rescues with specific details
- Verified acts of community support

Critical distinction: Must be documented and verifiable. "Random act of kindness" stories without verification don't qualify. The evidence matters.

### 10. Graphic Heavy Evidence

When to use: You have compelling primary source visuals (documents, photos, screenshots, receipts, objects) that should dominate the post.

What to extract:
- The single most viral insight the visual evidence proves
- Core contradiction, lie, or undeniable fact
- Axiom-like truth distilled to minimal text

Structure:
- Visual dominates (70%+ of image space)
- Minimal powerful text overlay
- Key phrases highlighted for emphasis
- Full detailed context in caption

How to express:
- Distill to axiom-like statement
- Often reveals a lie or contradiction
- Works WITH the visual, not explaining it
- Powerful framing in minimum words

Examples:
- Epstein passport: "His lawyers said never used. Stamps prove he did."
- Clinton painting: Student work to fundraiser to Epstein's wall
- Medical records showing systematic abuse
- Receipts proving contradictory claims

Critical skill: This is not "show the document and describe it." This is "distill the most viral truth the document proves into the most powerful minimal expression."

### 11. Cultural Resurgence (Then vs. Now)

When to use: Something from the past is measurably bigger, more popular, or more culturally relevant today than when it was originally released.

What to extract:
- The original release context and peak (chart position, box office, sales figure)
- The current peak using the same type of measurement
- The specific mechanism driving the resurgence (film placement, TikTok, cultural event)
- The human detail from the person most connected to the subject

Structure:
- Headline states the resurgence fact plainly
- Image text stacks origin fact, original peak, and current peak in consecutive paragraphs so the symmetry is visible on screen simultaneously
- Caption builds the full article: big picture opening, data layer, origin story, original success, resurgence drivers, comparison cases, what makes this case distinct, closing line

Examples:
- "Iris" by the Goo Goo Dolls peaking at #9 on Billboard in 1998 and #9 on Spotify's Global Daily Chart in 2025
- Kate Bush's Running Up That Hill becoming her biggest US hit 37 years after release via Stranger Things
- Fleetwood Mac's Dreams surging after the skateboarding TikTok video

Critical: The symmetry between the original peak and the current peak is the engine. Stack the two measurements in consecutive lines. Let the gap speak for itself. Never editorialize around the numbers.

### 12. Named Concept / Viral Theory

When to use: The most viral element of the source is not a claim or a quote but a named idea, pattern, or theory that the headline can state in full without explanation. The name alone creates immediate curiosity and recognition.

What to extract:
- The name of the concept or theory
- The specific facts or observations that make it feel true
- The emotional reason the concept resonates (grief, nostalgia, pattern recognition, identity)
- The origin of the theory (X thread, Reddit, forum, cultural moment)

Structure:
- Headline is the name only. No explanation. No credential. No claim.
- Image text delivers the facts that make the theory feel true, in the order that builds the most emotional weight
- Caption traces the origin, gives it scale (views, shares, reactions), covers why it is resurfacing now, ends on the line that re-opens the loop at a higher level

Examples:
- The Kobe Bryant End of the World Theory
- The Therian movement in Argentina
- Any named cultural phenomenon where the title alone stops the scroll

Critical distinction: The headline does no explaining. It only names. If the name requires explanation to be understood, it is not ready to be a headline. The test: does the name alone make someone stop and want to read more?

### 13. What This Actually Means For You

When to use: Source contains a genuine scientific, medical, or technical breakthrough that matters enormously to mainstream audiences but dies inside the language of the field it came from. The discovery is real and significant. The terminology is the barrier.

What to extract:
- The human implication, not the mechanism
- The before state (what everyone assumed was true or impossible)
- The moment that changed it
- What it means for a 45-year-old who has never heard the technical terms
- What it does NOT mean yet (critical for credibility)

Headline rules for this function:
- Never open with "Scientists have believed" or any variation. That is fluff. It signals a school report, not a revelation.
- Never use "last week" or any time marker that kills urgency. The finding is the urgency.
- Open with the assumption that is now broken. State it as fact, not as what scientists thought.
- Land on what changed and how far it went, using only numbers and plain language the mainstream mind can feel.
- The test: read the headline to someone who has never heard of the field. Do they immediately understand why it matters to their life? If not, rewrite.
- Best structure: "[What everyone assumed was permanent or true]. [What just happened to change it, in plain numbers or plain human stakes]."
- Real example: "Aging was always assumed to be a one-way road to death. An AI just produced results in human cells that scientists couldn't reach in 13 years of trying."

Structure:
- Headline translates the finding into plain human stakes. No jargon. No proper nouns that require prior knowledge.
- Image text builds the before/after arc in plain language. Start with what everyone believed. Show the moment it changed. Land on what just happened without using the technical name for it.
- Caption carries the technical layer for readers who want it, but only after the human stakes are established. Name the researchers, the institution, the mechanism. Be honest about what the results do not yet mean.

The translation rules:
- Replace every proper noun that requires prior knowledge with what it actually does ("Yamanaka factors" becomes "4 proteins that can reverse cellular aging")
- Replace every technical term with its human meaning ("pluripotency markers" becomes "biological signals that aging has reversed")
- Replace percentage improvements with analogies that make scale visceral ("50x improvement" becomes "the ceiling that held for 13 years moved in a single pass")
- State what it does NOT mean yet. Credibility comes from honesty about limits.

Why people share:
- Relief that something they feared was permanent might not be
- The feeling of being the first person in their circle to know something that matters
- Hope grounded in evidence, not inspiration

Examples:
- OpenAI improving cellular reprogramming efficiency 50x (aging research)
- Any medical breakthrough described in clinical language that has clear human implications
- Any scientific finding where the headline in the original source loses 90% of potential readers in the first sentence

Critical distinction: This is not dumbing down. It is translation. The finding stays accurate. The language becomes human. The test: does someone who has never heard of this field understand immediately why it matters to them personally?

### 14. Cultural Object Explainer

When to use: A piece of culture, a brand campaign, a film, an album, a fashion moment, a performance, has just landed and is already going viral or breaking. The post's job is not to reveal something hidden. It is to give the reader the full picture of why this moment matters, what it is in conversation with historically, and what is happening around it right now.

What to extract:
- What the object actually is and what happens in it (scene-by-scene if needed)
- The historical DNA it carries (what earlier moments it echoes, updates, or responds to)
- The cultural context around it right now (timing, adjacent stories, why this moment specifically)
- The reception (how the internet responded, what the dominant reactions were)
- Any product, product line, or concrete deliverable at the center of it

How this differs from other functions:
- Unlike Extract and Enrich, there is no single viral moment to present cleanly before enriching it. The entire object is the subject.
- Unlike Cultural Resurgence, this is not then vs. now symmetry. It is a new thing that carries the DNA of old things, and explaining that DNA is the whole post.
- Unlike Reveal Hidden Pattern, there is no hidden connection being surfaced. The connections are visible. The job is to explain why they matter.

Format:
- The article format works better than the standard image/caption format for this function. The object deserves a frame, a history, and a context layer that a single caption cannot hold.
- Use H2 headers to organize the key dimensions: the object itself, the homage or historical context, the product or concrete detail, the cultural timing.
- The headline should name the object and signal the cultural question it raises.
- The opening paragraph positions the object in the cultural moment before any detail is given.

Structure:
- Headline: names the object and the cultural question
- Opening paragraph: positions the moment, names the energy
- Section 1: what the object actually is, described specifically and vividly
- Section 2: the historical DNA, explained without over-referencing
- Section 3: the product, person, or concrete detail at the center
- Section 4: the cultural timing, why this landed now and not some other time

Why people share:
- The feeling of having the full picture when everyone around them only has part of it
- The pleasure of understanding why something feels important, not just that it does
- Being able to explain to someone else why a cultural moment matters

Examples:
- Dakota Johnson Spring 2026 Calvin Klein campaign
- A major album drop that carries the DNA of an earlier era
- A fashion campaign that deliberately echoes an iconic historical moment
- A film or series that reactivates cultural appetite for something thought to be gone

Critical distinction: The object is not a hook for a larger argument. The object is the subject. The post serves the object by giving it full context. Resist the urge to find a hidden pattern or a reveal. Sometimes a campaign is just a great campaign and the job is to explain exactly why.
