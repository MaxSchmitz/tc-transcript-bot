# Facebook Caption Writing

The image is an independent piece of content. So is the caption. They don't repeat each other. The caption expands the universe of the image — additional hooks, authority, evidence, and context the image couldn't hold — giving people a more immersive environment for the information using nothing but plain text.

**Format rule: Plain text only. No bullet points, no bold, no headers, no formatting of any kind.**

---

## The Opening Line

The opening line is its own decision every time. It has four viable forms.

**The emotional agreement opener.** A short, punchy statement of feeling or observation that the reader will immediately recognize as true. It creates alignment before the facts land. Use it when the subject matter is visceral enough that the reader's gut reaction is itself the hook.

> Example: The Epstein neuroscience post opened with "This feels like it's straight out of a horror movie." One sentence. The reader agrees before they've read a single fact. Then the document evidence lands on top of that agreement.

**The authority signal opener.** The name, credential, or institution that makes the image's claim real. Use it when the image makes a dramatic claim and the reader needs to know immediately that it's grounded.

> Example: The Cobain post opened with the researchers' names and the journal: "Michelle Wilkins and forensic specialist Brian Burnett led the team that published these findings in the International Journal of Forensic Sciences." The image said "forensic team." The caption made that real.

**The context opener.** Use it when the image contains a person or event the audience won't fully recognize without grounding first.

> Example: The Harumi/Chalamet post opened with who Dan Harumi is before touching the Chalamet story at all. "Dan Harumi is a New York-based comedian and content creator known for these long-form hot-take breakdowns. He dropped this one on March 10 after specifically saying he's a Chalamet hater." The reader now understands the frame before they encounter the argument.

**The reaction opener.** A statement or question built from the sharpest public reaction to the story. Grok analysis surfaces these — viral tweets, top comments, meme-level restatements of the core tension. The tweet is research, not source material. Never quote it, name the user, or reference the platform. Extract the underlying insight and rephrase it as a clean opener in your own voice.

> Example: Grok surfaced a viral tweet calling the Blockbuster/Google deal "the original Netflix/YouTube combo." The caption opened with: "Blockbuster and Google were one deal away from becoming Netflix and YouTube before either company existed." The tweet found the angle. The opener delivered it fresh.

What the opening never does: restate the headline, announce what the caption is about to explain, or tell the reader how to feel about what they're going to read.

---

## The Body

Give the reader the specific evidence the image couldn't hold. Document numbers. Verbatim quotes. Named sources. Specific figures. The difference between a body that works and one that doesn't is almost always specificity.

> Example: The image on the Cobain post said "heroin levels that would have left Cobain unable to move." The caption gave the actual number: "The toxicology number at the center of their argument is 1.52 mg/L of blood morphine. That figure comes directly from the 1994 autopsy and isn't disputed." The abstract claim in the image became a verifiable fact in the caption.

Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.

**When something in the image will be misread, address it directly.** Name the misread before the reader makes it. Don't defend the post. Give the reader the tools to understand it accurately.

> Example: The Kahn deposition post contained a line about a Trump accuser receiving money from the Epstein survivor fund. That line would be misread as hush money from Trump. The caption addressed it head-on: "One of the more misread details coming out of this deposition involves a Trump accuser and the survivor fund." Then it explained exactly what the fund was, who created it, who it paid, and why a Trump accuser could be among the recipients.

**Repetition from the image is allowed** when it keeps the narrative coherent. The caption may be the first thing some readers encounter. Restating a key fact in service of coherence is not redundancy — but restating it because the writer couldn't think of what else to say is.

**Identification is mandatory** for every name that isn't a household name.

> Example: "Steven Sinofsky" means nothing. "Steven Sinofsky, the former president of Microsoft's Windows Division, who left Microsoft in 2012" means something.

**Verbatim quotes earn their place** when the exact language is the point. Don't paraphrase when the original wording carries more weight than your restatement.

> Example: The Epstein neuroscience caption quoted Bock's definition verbatim: "the class of intentional behaviors directed upon changing the beliefs of an agent in a way that the deceiver does not himself consider factual." That is dense academic language describing manipulation. Paraphrasing would have dissolved it.

## The Close

The close is where the caption earns its place or doesn't.

The close should leave something open. Not a summary of what was just said. Not a moral. Not an instruction to the reader about how to feel. The facts do that work. The close either creates unresolved tension, reframes what came before, or lands a truth the body was building toward that the reader now sees clearly.

> Example: The Cobain caption closed with the SPD quote: "This continues to be the position held by this department... we've seen nothing to date that would warrant re-opening." The image reads as settled. The SPD quote leaves it unsettled.

> Example: The Chalamet/Harumi caption closed with a top comment from the video: "The Timmy Discourse is the most ballet and opera have been discussed in the mainstream in many decades. And that's exactly what his point was." The caption didn't editorialize on whether Harumi was right. A stranger's observation did that work, and it landed cleaner than any closing argument would have.

> Example: The Epstein neuroscience caption closed with: "The emails are real. The question Epstein asked, and the plants he was growing, are part of the same public record." No commentary. Two facts placed side by side close the loop.

> Example: The Bridgerton caption closed with: "He got too big for the Ton." One sentence. The insight was already in the body. The close names it at a higher level without restating it.

---

## What the Caption Never Does

**Never tells the reader how to feel.** The facts produce the reaction. Announcing the reaction before the facts land kills it.

**Never explains an insight the reader just formed on their own.**

> Example (what to cut): In the Chalamet caption, the original draft included: "not because his acting ability changed, but because our love for him evolved." The sentence before it already delivered the insight. The explanation was the reader's job, not the writer's.

**Never editorializes around facts that speak for themselves.**

> Example (what to cut): "The internet did not miss that" and "That framing matters" were both cut from the Chalamet caption. The reader encounters the fact and forms the irony on their own. Pointing at it dissolves it.

**Never uses a triple construction where the third element deflates the first two.**

> Example (what not to do): "He became larger than life. Larger than the Ton. Larger than those mutton chops from Season 1." The first two land. The third announces it's being clever and undercuts both.

**Never lands on a soft closing sentiment when the facts already provided a stronger one.**

> Example (what to cut): The Bridgerton caption originally ended with "But they'll still live on in a corner of our hearts." The post didn't need a soft landing. "He got too big for the Ton" was already the close.

If removing a sentence makes the paragraph stronger, the sentence was hurting it.

---

## The Underlying Principle

The caption lets the facts carry the reaction. Every strong edit is some version of removing a line that told the reader what to think. The reader forms the conclusion. The caption gives them everything they need to get there and then gets out of the way.
