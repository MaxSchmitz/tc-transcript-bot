# Caption Writing

The caption assumes the headline and image have already done their job. The reader has stopped scrolling. Now the caption earns the share.

---

## What the Caption Does

1. **Adds depth** -- context, evidence, authority that the headline couldn't carry
2. **Earns trust** -- specific names, dates, sources, credentials make the reader believe
3. **Triggers the share** -- the caption is what makes someone think "my friend needs to see this"

The caption does NOT rescue a weak headline. If removing the caption makes the post incomprehensible, the headline failed.

---

## Structure

### When the image has a strong headline:

Open with context. The hook has already landed.

```
[Context sentence grounding the reader in the story]
[Evidence layer: specific facts, quotes, data]
[Supporting detail that deepens the story]
[Closing line: forward-looking, provocative, or resonant]
```

### When the image has no headline:

Open with a viral hook. The caption IS the hook.

```
[Hook: the single most compelling fact or quote]
[Context: what happened, who was involved]
[Evidence: supporting details, data, sources]
[Close]
```

---

## Rules

### Length
- 3-6 sentences for most posts
- Can go longer (8-10 sentences) for complex stories with multiple evidence layers
- Never pad. Every sentence must add information or emotional weight.

### Tone
- Conversational but authoritative
- Write like a smart friend explaining something at a bar, not a journalist filing copy
- Contractions always (he's, they're, it's)
- Short sentences. One idea per sentence.
- No jargon unless the audience expects it

### Specificity
- Names over categories ("Dolly Parton" not "a celebrity")
- Numbers over adjectives ("$437" not "hundreds of dollars")
- Dates over vagueness ("in 2003" not "years ago")
- Quotes over paraphrase when the original words are strong

### What to include
- Attribution and sourcing (where did this come from?)
- The "so what" -- why this matters beyond the immediate story
- Authority signals (court documents, peer-reviewed studies, named experts)
- The detail that didn't fit in the headline but makes the story richer

### What to cut
- Anything that repeats the headline without adding new information
- Setup sentences ("Let me explain..." / "Here's the thing...")
- Engagement bait ("Let that sink in" / "Read that again")
- Editorializing that the facts already handle ("Shocking" / "Heartbreaking")
- Calls to action ("Share this!" / "Tag someone who...")

## Before/After Examples

**Before:** "This is absolutely insane. A teacher spent her own money on coats for her students. In today's economy, teachers shouldn't have to do this. Let that sink in. Share if you agree."

**After:** "Her salary is $34,000 a year. She spent $437 on winter coats because 22 of her 24 students didn't have one. The school district's clothing assistance budget was $0."

Why: The before version editorializes, uses dead phrases, and asks for engagement. The after version stacks facts that produce the same emotions organically.

---

**Before:** "Harvey Weinstein gave his first interview from prison and what he said will shock you. Here's everything you need to know about this exclusive sit-down."

**After:** "Weinstein's first prison interview. 73 years old, wheelchair-bound, 23 hours a day in his cell at Rikers. He still insists he never assaulted anyone. His retrial starts April 14. Over 80 women disagree."

Why: The before version promises and delays. The after version delivers immediately -- age, condition, schedule, claim, date, counterpoint. Every clause is a fact.

---

## The Share Test

Before publishing, ask: would someone screenshot this caption and send it to a friend? If the answer is no, the caption is either too generic or too long. Cut until it's worth sharing.
