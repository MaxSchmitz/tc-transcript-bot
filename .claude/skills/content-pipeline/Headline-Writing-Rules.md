# Headline Writing

The headline is the only part of the post that competes with every other piece of content on the feed. It has one job: make someone stop scrolling. Everything else depends on the headline doing its job first.

---

## The Core Principle: Specificity Over Formula

Generic headlines signal "content." Specific headlines signal "information worth stopping for."

The test: could this headline apply to 10 different stories? If yes, it's generic. A good headline could only describe one story.

---

## Before/After Examples

### 1. Lead with the finding, not the method

**Before:** "Researchers followed strippers for 2 cycles and found that ovulating women earned more."
**After:** "Women who were ovulating earned $335 a shift. Women who weren't earned $185. The men had no idea why."

Why: "Researchers found" is invisible. The numbers are the news. The contrast between $335 and $185 stops the scroll. The kicker ("The men had no idea why") adds a curiosity gap without being clickbait.

**Before:** "A new study found that clear-air turbulence is increasing."
**After:** "Clear-air turbulence has increased 55% since 1979. It's invisible to radar and by 2050 it could more than double."

Why: "A new study found" adds zero information. The 55% increase since 1979 is concrete and surprising. The radar detail adds fear. The 2050 projection creates urgency.

**Before:** "Scientists discovered a link between social media use and teen depression."
**After:** "Girls who used social media for 5+ hours a day were 3 times more likely to be diagnosed with depression. Boys showed almost no effect."

Why: The gendered split is the actual finding. "Scientists discovered a link" could be about anything. The 5-hour threshold and 3x multiplier are specific enough to make someone react.

---

### 2. Never start with How/Why/What/The

These words are invisible on a feed. They signal explanation, not revelation.

**Before:** "How one man turned a $200 investment into a $50 million company"
**After:** "He put $200 into a vending machine at a laundromat in 2003. The company is now worth $50 million."

Why: "How" promises a process. The specific details ($200, vending machine, laundromat, 2003) create a scene. The jump from laundromat to $50 million is the hook.

**Before:** "Why doctors are warning against a popular sleep supplement"
**After:** "Melatonin gummies sold for children contain up to 347% more melatonin than the label says."

Why: "Why doctors are warning" is vague authority. 347% over the label amount is a specific, alarming fact that makes parents stop immediately.

**Before:** "The surprising history of Coca-Cola"
**After:** "Coca-Cola originally contained 9 milligrams of cocaine per serving. The company removed it in 1903 but kept the coca leaf extract."

Why: "Surprising history" is a promise. The cocaine content and 1903 date are deliverables. The headline delivers instead of promising.

---

### 3. Use the most specific human detail

**Before:** "A teacher's act of kindness went viral"
**After:** "A kindergarten teacher in Oklahoma spent $437 of her own money on winter coats for every kid in her class. Her salary is $34,000 a year."

Why: $437, winter coats, every kid, $34,000 salary. The ratio of sacrifice to income is the emotional engine.

**Before:** "Celebrity donates to struggling family"
**After:** "Dolly Parton found out a Tennessee family lost their house in a wildfire. She wrote them a check for $1,000 a month for 6 months."

Why: Name, state, specific disaster, exact dollar amount, exact duration. Every generic word is replaced with a fact.

---

### 4. The concrete detail that stops the scroll

**Before:** "Prison conditions in America are worse than people think"
**After:** "Harvey Weinstein sits in a Rikers cell 23 hours a day, reading memoirs by people who no longer speak to him."

Why: 23 hours, Rikers, memoirs by former friends. The image is instant and specific.

**Before:** "AI is advancing faster than expected"
**After:** "An AI produced results in human cells that scientists couldn't reach in 13 years of trying. It took one pass."

Why: "13 years vs. one pass" is the contrast that creates the headline.

**Before:** "Famous musician's song is experiencing a resurgence"
**After:** "The Goo Goo Dolls released 'Iris' in 1998 and it peaked at #9 on Billboard. In 2025 it hit #9 on Spotify's Global Daily Chart."

Why: Same song, same chart position, 27 years apart. The symmetry IS the headline.

---

### 5. Synthesize what the facts mean, don't report what happened

If the facts are not shocking, radical, or breaking on their own, don't report them. Synthesize up. Tell the reader what the events add up to before you tell them what happened.

The test: do these facts stop someone cold on their own? If yes, lead with them. If not, the headline needs to tell the reader what the facts mean before they'll care what happened.

**Before:** "Blockbuster's board signed a joint venture with Google to bundle paid streaming with YouTube. Google's board was ready to sign. Then someone leaked a bankruptcy rumor to the LA Times."

**After:** "Blockbuster had a deal with Google that could have kept it alive through the Netflix era. It collapsed at the last minute."

Why: The before version is a plot summary. Three accurate facts reported in sequence. But accurate isn't the same as meaningful. Nobody feels the stakes from a board signing and a leaked rumor. The after version tells the reader what those events add up to first. The reader understands the stakes immediately, before they know a single procedural detail. That's what makes them want the rest of the story.

Exception: when the facts are genuinely shocking, radical, or breaking on their own, lead with them directly. The synthesis rule applies when the facts need context to land, not when the facts are the entire point.

---

### 6. Don't editorialize what the facts already say

**Before:** "Shocking new details emerge in Epstein case"
**After:** "Epstein's personal pilot testified that he flew Bill Clinton, Prince Andrew, and Kevin Spacey to the island. He remembered the names because he logged every passenger."

Why: The names are the shock. "Shocking new details" tells the reader how to feel before giving them the reason.

**Before:** "Heartbreaking interview with incarcerated Hollywood mogul"
**After:** "2 of Harvey Weinstein's daughters changed their last names and haven't spoken to him in 6 years."

Why: The fact is already heartbreaking (or satisfying, depending on the reader). Labeling it reduces its impact.

## Banned Openers

Never open a headline with:

- "Researchers found/discovered/say..."
- "A study showed/found..."
- "Scientists discovered/say..."
- "New data suggests/shows..."
- "According to a study/report..."
- "How..." / "Why..." / "What..." / "The..."
- "Here's what happened when..."
- "You won't believe..."
- "Breaking:"

The finding leads. Always.

Exception: when a title, name, or property is load-bearing, it belongs in the headline. "In Eyes Wide Shut..." is not setup. It's the reason the reader stops. The ban on "The..." or "In X..." openers applies to filler framing, not to context that makes the rest of the headline matter. If removing the name or title means the reader has no reason to care, it stays.

---

## Structural Rules

- Numbers as digits, not words (35, not thirty-five)
- No em dashes
- Short. Under 25 words is ideal. Under 15 is better.
- One sentence or two short sentences. Never three. If the most surprising thing is in the third sentence, the headline failed. That's caption structure masquerading as a headline. The finding leads. The body handles the buildup.
- If using a quote, it must be the most specific, surprising line -- not a generic statement
- If the best headline is a question, it must be one the reader desperately wants answered -- never rhetorical

---

## The Litmus Test

Read the headline to someone who knows nothing about the topic. Do they immediately want to know more? If they shrug, the headline failed. If they say "wait, what?" it works.
